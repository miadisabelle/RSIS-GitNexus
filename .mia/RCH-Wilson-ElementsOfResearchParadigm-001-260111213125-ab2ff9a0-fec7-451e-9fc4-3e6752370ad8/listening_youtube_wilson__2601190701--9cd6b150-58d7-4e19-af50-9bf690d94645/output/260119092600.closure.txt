NARRATIVE BEATS & CONCEPTS
1. Relational Ontology & Reality
1.1. Reality as interconnected points of light (pinpricks) joined by filaments
1.2. Physical world emerges from relationships between entities, not entities themselves
1.3. Everything possesses spirit - land, knowledge, ideas, matter
1.4. Relationality extends through time and space (ancestors, future generations)

2. Indigenous Research Paradigm
2.1. Research as ceremony - deliberate entry into sacred space
2.2. Mana: sacred space between things in relationship
2.3. Relational accountability - responsibility to all relationships
2.4. Knowledge as relationship, not property to acquire or explore

3. Ceremonial Research Stages
3.1. Live congruent lifestyle before beginning
3.2. Prepare the space (planning, ethics, gathering materials)
3.3. Bring everyone together with intention
3.4. Engage in ritual (systematic inquiry)
3.5. Achieve enlightenment/understanding
3.6. Incorporate knowledge into lifestyle (wisdom)

4. Wetiko Disease & Decolonization
4.1. Spiritual crash - disconnection from relationships
4.2. Greed, consumption, malignant ego
4.3. Protections: laughter, pure willpower, community connection
4.4. Epistemicide - killing of knowledge systems

5. Cultural Iceberg Model
5.1. Visible culture (actions, dress, ceremonies)
5.2. Underlying philosophy (values, beliefs, ways of being)
5.3. Core identity remains despite surface changes

STORY SYNOPSIS
Title: "The Filament Keeper"

Dr. Sienna Okonkwo, a burned-out climate scientist, discovers her grandmother's journals revealing an Indigenous Nigerian cosmology where reality is woven filaments of light. After her research on coral reef collapse is dismissed for lacking "objectivity," she returns to her ancestral village where elder Nnamdi teaches her to "see the threads." Sienna learns research isn't extraction but relationship-building. She develops a revolutionary methodology documenting reef ecosystems as living relationships, facing institutional resistance while her former colleague Marcus attempts to patent her grandmother's knowledge. The climax occurs when Sienna must choose between academic validation and protecting sacred knowledge, ultimately creating a new research collective that bridges Indigenous and Western science through "filament mapping" - a practice recognizing all entities as interconnected spirits.

THREE-ACT STRUCTURE

ACT I: Disconnection

Opening: Sienna's research rejected, marriage failing, disconnected from family
Inciting incident: Grandmother's death, inheritance of journals
First plot point: Returns to Nigerian village, meets Elder Nnamdi
Establishes Sienna's spiritual crash - consumed by career, isolated
ACT II: Relationship Building

Sienna learns relational ontology through ceremonies
Develops "filament mapping" - visualizing ecosystem relationships as light networks
Midpoint: Breakthrough showing coral reefs as relational beings, not objects
Marcus discovers her work, attempts appropriation
Institutional ethics board challenges her methodology
Low point: Rejected by both academic world and initially by village elders (hasn't proven commitment)
ACT III: Integration

Sienna performs ceremony with community, demonstrating lived commitment
Creates research collective with Indigenous knowledge keepers
Confronts Marcus, chooses protection over publication
Resolution: Establishes alternative validation system, repairs marriage through relational accountability
New normal: Teaches "ceremonial research" bridging knowledge systems
WORLD BUILDING

Setting: Contemporary Nigeria/UK Split

Village of Umunna: Coastal community maintaining pre-colonial knowledge systems, stone circles for gathering, elder council system
Imperial College London: Prestigious institution with rigid research protocols, ethics boards, competitive grant culture
The Reef Laboratory: Where Sienna conducted original research, sterile environment contrasting with living ecosystems
Sacred Grove: Where ceremonies occur, describe
